01

The Agricultural Landscape

Australia's farm output is spread unevenly across the continent. New South Wales and Victoria dominate by total value, but each state has its own agricultural identity — shaped by climate, land, and tradition.

Total Agricultural Value by State (2021–22)

Darker shading indicates higher total farm-gate value. Hover over each state to see its total value and top-producing commodity. New South Wales leads with $19.6B; Queensland's output is dominated by cattle, which account for over half its total farm value.

Key insight

New South Wales and Victoria together produce ~49% of Australia's total agricultural output, driven by their diverse mix of grains, livestock, and horticulture.

Top Commodities by National Value (2021–22)

Cattle & calves lead at $15.3B, followed by wheat at $13.1B. Together, beef and grains account for nearly 40% of all farm output. Each bar is coloured by its broad category — orange for broadacre crops, vermilion for livestock slaughter.

Three Decades of Crop Growth

Australian crop production has expanded and diversified dramatically since 1989. The rise of canola and the resilience of wheat tell the story of a sector adapting to changing global demand — and surviving punishing droughts.

"Australian farmers now plant more canola than at any time in history — a crop that was barely grown here 35 years ago."

Area Planted to Key Crops (Australia, 1989–2026)

Wheat dominates by area, though it fluctuates sharply with drought and prices. Canola has risen steadily to become Australia's second-largest crop by area — a remarkable transformation over just 35 years. Hover for exact values.

Drought impact

03

Getting More From Every Hectare

Australian farmers are producing significantly more food from the same land. Advances in crop varieties, precision agriculture, and better water management have driven yields upward across all states — even through drought years.

Wheat Yield: 1990s vs 2020s Average (t/ha)

Every state improved its average wheat yield. Tasmania — with its cooler, wetter climate — achieved the largest absolute gain, reaching 6.7 t/ha. Values are labelled directly on the chart.

Wheat Yield Over Time (Australia, t/ha)

Despite year-to-year volatility driven by drought, the long-term trend in wheat yield is clearly upward — from around 1.6 t/ha in 1990 to over 2.8 t/ha by 2021–22. Two major drought episodes are marked.

Wheat: Yield vs Year, Bubble Size = Area Planted (1990–2025)

Each bubble is one growing season. Orange bubbles mark drought years (yield < 1.6 t/ha); blue bubbles are good seasons. Bubble size shows how much land was planted. The dashed trend line confirms steady yield improvement regardless of area planted — demonstrating it is efficiency, not just more land, driving output growth.

Efficiency insight

Some recent seasons planted less land than the mid-2000s yet achieved double the yield per hectare. Precision agriculture and improved varieties are the real driver — not simply planting more ground.

04

The Economics of Australian Farming

Agriculture is not just about what is grown — it is about whether farmers can make a living and whether Australia can compete on the world stage. The past 30 years have seen strong growth in both farm income and exports.

Farmers' Terms of Trade (Index, 2025–26 = 100)

The terms of trade measures what farmers earn (prices received) relative to what they pay (input costs). A rising index means farming is becoming more profitable. After decades of decline, the index recovered — rising from 2015 to a peak around 2020–21 as global demand surged. It has since eased back toward the base level as input costs rose, with forecasts showing continued softening.

Gross Value of Farm Production ($M, 1994–2027)

Total farm production value has grown from $24B in 1994–95 to over $88B in 2021–22. Crops (orange) have consistently exceeded meat & slaughter (vermilion) throughout — with the gap widening sharply after 2020 as record grain harvests drove strong growth. The dashed line shows the overall total.

Australian Agricultural Exports by Category ($M, 1994–2027)

Agricultural exports have grown from under $20B in 1994–95 to over $78B in 2022–23. Meat (vermilion) consistently dominates, while grains/oilseeds/pulses (orange) have surged recently. Dairy (green), horticulture (purple), and wool (yellow) each contribute a smaller but steady share.

Global reach

Australia exports the equivalent of ~70% of what it produces on farm, making it one of the world's most trade-exposed agricultural nations. Asia is the dominant destination — particularly for beef, wheat, and wool.
